01

The California Exodus

California led the nation in outbound migration. Los Angeles County alone saw over 70,000 households file their taxes from a different county the following year.

02

Where Did They Go?

Texas, Nevada, and Arizona were the top destinations. Clark County (Las Vegas) gained over 10,000 net households, while Texas suburbs like Collin and Williamson counties saw explosive growth.

03

The Local Surprise

Most moves weren’t cross-country. The top migration corridor? LA to Orange County—just 30 miles. 21,267 households made that short hop, taking $2.4 billion in income.

04

Following the Money

Income didn’t move equally. High earners disproportionately left high-tax states. Middlesex County (Boston) lost $2 billion in AGI—despite “only” losing 8,844 net returns.

05

Winners & Losers

Top gainers: Florida’s Polk County (+11,721), Nevada’s Clark County (+10,067). Top losers: Philadelphia (-9,643), San Diego (-8,622). The Sunbelt surge is real.

06

The Remote Work Effect

When offices closed, tech hubs emptied. San Francisco lost households to Austin, Denver, and Boise. Silicon Valley’s outflow funded a dozen new tech scenes.

07

The Retirement Belt

Follow the snowbirds. Northern counties send a steady stream to Florida and Arizona. Maricopa County (Phoenix) alone gained $1.2 billion in retirement income.

08

The Boomerang

Migration isn’t one-way. For every corridor, there’s a reverse flow. LA to SF and SF to LA trade nearly equal volumes. The American shuffle never stops.
